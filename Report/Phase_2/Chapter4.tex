\chapter{\uppercase{Experimental Evaluation}}
% Page numbering handled by Chapter 1

This chapter evaluates each component and the end-to-end pipeline empirically, addressing two questions: does the GNN meet its classification and conformal prediction targets (Sections~\ref{sec:gnn-evolution}--\ref{sec:conformal}), and does the cascade reduce expensive LLM calls while maintaining full finding resolution (Section~\ref{sec:cascade-eval})?

% 4.1 EXPERIMENTAL SETUP
% =========================================================================
\section{\uppercase{Experimental Setup}}
\label{sec:setup}

All GNN training ran on Google Colab with a single NVIDIA Tesla T4 GPU (16\,GB VRAM), making our pipeline reproducible without institutional GPU access. Inference and cascade orchestration ran on a consumer workstation (Intel Core i5-12400, 16\,GB RAM). The software stack comprises PyTorch 2.1, PyTorch Geometric 2.4, GraphCodeBERT (125\,M parameters) for embeddings, TorchCP 0.2 for conformal prediction, and Google Gemini 2.5 Flash as the primary LLM provider.

\subsection{Datasets}

The GNN training corpus aggregates seven public vulnerability datasets spanning five languages. Table~\ref{tab:datasets} summarizes each source. We applied three data-quality controls: (1)~MD5 content-hash deduplication, removing 1,326 duplicates from the 41\,K raw pool; (2)~a minimum-length filter ($\geq 3$~lines, $\geq 20$~characters); and (3)~strict per-language 1:1 class balancing via undersampling. The resulting 21,150 graphs were split 60/15/15/10 into training (12,689), validation, calibration, and test partitions using stratified sampling.

C/C++ dominates at 94.6\%, reflecting the state of publicly available vulnerability datasets. VUDENC and CVEfixes exhibit extreme class imbalance (84/2,916 and 36/2,964 vulnerable/safe respectively), so only $\sim$84 and $\sim$36 balanced pairs survive after undersampling; despite this, the addition of these real-world Python samples proved significant in V5 (Section~\ref{sec:gnn-evolution}). The top CWEs are CWE-119 (buffer overflow, 2,178 samples), CWE-20 (input validation, 1,805), CWE-125 (out-of-bounds read, 1,064), and CWE-264 (permissions, 857); 5,450 samples (25.8\%) carry no CWE label. PrimeVul~\cite{primevul2024}, a rigorously deduplicated C/C++ corpus, failed to load due to HuggingFace metadata incompatibilities; its absence is a limitation we address in Section~\ref{sec:discussion}.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

We report standard binary classification metrics for the GNN:
\textbf{F1} (harmonic mean of precision and recall),
\textbf{precision} (fraction of flagged code that is truly vulnerable),
\textbf{recall} (fraction of vulnerable code that is flagged), and
\textbf{AUC-ROC} (threshold-independent discriminative ability).
We prioritize F1 over accuracy because the class-balanced dataset (1:1 ratio)
makes accuracy less informative; F1 captures the precision-recall trade-off
that directly affects false positive rates in deployment.
We report AUC-ROC following the convention in vulnerability detection
literature~\cite{devign2019, primevul2024}.
For conformal prediction, we report \textbf{coverage}
($\geq 1 - \alpha = 90\%$ target), \textbf{singleton rate}
(prediction sets containing exactly one class, the primary efficiency metric),
and \textbf{mean set size}.
For the cascade, we report per-stage \textbf{resolution rate} and
total \textbf{LLM call reduction} relative to a full-LLM baseline.

\section{\uppercase{GNN Classification Results}}
\label{sec:gnn-evolution}

We trained five model versions across four weeks, each addressing a failure mode discovered in its predecessor. Table~\ref{tab:gnn-evolution} tracks the progression from V2 through V5. V1 is excluded because it trained exclusively on synthetic Juliet data and produced deceptively inflated metrics (F1$\approx$0.72) that collapsed on cross-dataset evaluation, consistent with PrimeVul's observation of models dropping from 90\%+ to 3.09\% on realistic data~\cite{primevul2024}.

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\small
\caption{GNN model evolution: V2 through V5 on held-out test data.}
\label{tab:gnn-evolution}
\begin{tabular}{@{}llrrrrrr@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Ver.} & \textbf{Architecture} & \textbf{Params} & \textbf{Samples} & \textbf{F1} & \textbf{Prec.} & \textbf{Rec.} & \textbf{AUC} \\
\midrule
V2 & Mini-GAT (2L, 4H) & 298\,K & 1,700 & 0.560 & 0.397 & 0.951 & 0.744 \\
V3 & MiniGINv3 (3L) & 2.4\,M & 3,032 & 0.653 & 0.544 & 0.815 & 0.623 \\
V4 & MiniGINv3 (3L) & 2.4\,M & 20,928 & \textbf{0.781} & 0.675 & 0.926 & \textbf{0.826} \\
V5 & MiniGINv3 + ConfTS & 2.4\,M & 21,150 & 0.750 & 0.643 & 0.899 & 0.781 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\textbf{V2 (GAT Baseline).} The 2-layer GAT with 298\,K parameters achieved F1\,=\,0.560 with precision of 0.397, predicting nearly every sample as vulnerable (recall\,=\,0.951). GAT's non-injective weighted-mean aggregation conflated structurally distinct neighborhoods~\cite{xu2019gin}, and stacking Focal Loss~\cite{focalloss2017} ($\gamma=2$) on class-weight oversampling over-suppressed the safe-class gradient. Despite weak classification, 0.744 AUC-ROC confirmed that GraphCodeBERT embeddings contain discriminative signal. Per-language analysis revealed that Python (F1\,=\,0.633) and JavaScript (F1\,=\,0.650) outperformed C/C++ (F1\,=\,0.543), likely because the smaller sub-corpora contained more homogeneous patterns. The degenerate precision validated the cascade design: a standalone GNN with 60\% false positive rate would be unusable, but as a filter within the cascade, even moderate discrimination provides routing value.

\textbf{V3 (GIN Architecture).} Replacing GAT with a 3-layer Graph Isomorphism Network~\cite{xu2019gin} yielded a \textbf{+17\% absolute F1 improvement} (0.560 $\rightarrow$ 0.653). GIN's sum-aggregation is provably as expressive as the 1-Weisfeiler-Leman test, preserving structural distinctions that GAT conflated. However, V3 trained on only 3,032 graphs due to a configuration bug that capped per-language maximums at 3,000, silently discarding over 20,000 available C/C++ samples. The 2.4\,M-parameter model on 1,819 training samples (0.76 samples per thousand parameters) was deep in overfitting territory, reaching best validation F1 at epoch 20 of 45. V3 also introduced mean pooling (replacing CLS-token extraction) and a \texttt{language\_id} feature, both of which persisted through later versions. The conformal predictor produced only 0.22\% singletons. Data starvation, not architecture, was the primary bottleneck.

\textbf{V4 (Data Expansion).} Fixing the data cap unlocked a 7$\times$ training data increase (1,819 $\rightarrow$ 12,556 samples), producing a \textbf{+20\% absolute F1 improvement} (0.653 $\rightarrow$ 0.781), the single most impactful change across the entire training history. V4 achieved AUC-ROC of 0.826, recall of 0.926, and strong per-CWE performance: CWE-476 (null pointer) F1\,=\,0.926, CWE-787 (out-of-bounds write) 0.895, CWE-416 (use-after-free) 0.872, and CWE-362 (race condition) 0.800. Memory safety CWEs cluster above 0.87, consistent with the hypothesis that graph representations capture pointer and memory access patterns effectively. V4 is competitive with published detectors: Devign reports 0.637 accuracy~\cite{devign2019}, ReGVD achieves 0.68 F1~\cite{regvd2022}, and LineVul reaches 0.72 F1~\cite{linevul2022}. We do not claim state-of-the-art; the GNN operates as a \emph{filter stage}, not a standalone classifier, and its value lies in the conformal prediction layer built on top.

\textbf{V5 (Conformal Integration).} V5 made two changes that sacrificed 3.1 percentage points of F1 (0.781 $\rightarrow$ 0.750)
in exchange for enabling conformal prediction.
First, removing label smoothing (0.1 $\rightarrow$ 0.0) allowed logit magnitudes
to grow unconstrained, producing a bimodal probability distribution with clear
separation between safe and vulnerable samples.
With label smoothing, both classes clustered around 0.55, making the optimal
threshold close to 0.5.
Without it, safe samples concentrate near 0.0 and vulnerable samples near 1.0,
shifting the F1-optimal decision threshold from 0.53 (V4) to 0.32 (V5),
selected via grid search on the validation set.
Second, Conformal Temperature Scaling (ConfTS)~\cite{dabah2024} was integrated
as a post-hoc calibration layer. V5 also incorporated VUDENC and CVEfixes, adding 397 Python samples that raised Python F1 from 0.667 to \textbf{0.836}. The model trained for 61 epochs (best at epoch 41) in 7.0 minutes on the T4. The cascade does not require the GNN to be a perfect classifier; it requires the GNN to \emph{know when it is wrong}, which is what conformal prediction measures. Four lessons emerged: (1)~data matters more than architecture (7$\times$ data yielded +20\% F1 vs.\ +17\% from GAT-to-GIN); (2)~synthetic benchmarks produce misleadingly high metrics; (3)~label smoothing and conformal prediction are fundamentally incompatible; and (4)~offline conformal calibration requires deployment-specific recalibration.

\section{\uppercase{Conformal Prediction Analysis}}
\label{sec:conformal}

The conformal prediction layer converts GNN outputs into discrete routing decisions: a singleton prediction set $\{$safe$\}$ or $\{$vulnerable$\}$ resolves the finding at Stage~2, while an ambiguous set $\{$safe, vulnerable$\}$ escalates to Stage~3. Adaptive Prediction Sets (APS)~\cite{angelopoulos2023} provide a distribution-free coverage guarantee: $P(\text{true label} \in C(X)) \geq 1 - \alpha$, where $\alpha = 0.1$ targets 90\% coverage.

\subsection{The Calibration Challenge: V2--V4 Produced 0\% Singletons}

For binary APS with $\alpha = 0.1$, a singleton set requires the model to rank the true class first \emph{and} assign sufficiently high probability. If more than 10\% of calibration samples are misranked, the 90th-percentile quantile reaches 1.0, forcing both classes into every prediction set. Accuracy alone does not explain the failure.
A model with 74\% accuracy \emph{could} produce singletons if its correct
predictions were highly confident.
V4's label smoothing trained against soft targets [0.933, 0.067], capping
the maximum logit gap at approximately 2.6 nats and compressing all softmax
outputs into 0.50--0.60.
Even correctly classified samples produced APS scores of $\sim$0.55, while
misclassified samples scored 1.0.
This reveals a tension between classification regularization and conformal
prediction: label smoothing prevents overconfident predictions~\cite{guocalibration2017},
while conformal prediction \emph{requires} them to distinguish confident
from uncertain outputs.
Practitioners must choose which to optimize for; we chose conformal.

\subsection{The ConfTS Solution}

Conformal Temperature Scaling (ConfTS)~\cite{dabah2024} resolves this through post-hoc calibration, applying a single temperature parameter $T$ to the softmax:

\begin{equation}
    \text{calibrated\_probs} = \text{softmax}(\mathbf{z} / T)
    \label{eq:ch4-confts}
\end{equation}

Unlike standard temperature scaling~\cite{guocalibration2017} (which finds $T > 1$ to soften overconfident predictions), ConfTS minimizes mean APS prediction set size while maintaining coverage. For our underconfident model, ConfTS finds $T < 1$, sharpening the softmax. We searched 75 candidates $T \in [0.05, 3.0]$ on the validation set. Table~\ref{tab:confts-grid} reports representative operating points.

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\small
\caption{ConfTS grid search on the V5 validation set. Selected: $T = 0.10$ (minimum $T$ meeting 90\% coverage).}
\label{tab:confts-grid}
\begin{tabular}{@{}rrrr@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Temp ($T$)} & \textbf{Val Coverage (\%)} & \textbf{Singleton (\%)} & \textbf{Mean Set Size} \\
\midrule
0.05 & 87.1 & 66.2 & 1.338 \\
\textbf{0.10} & \textbf{92.0} & \textbf{53.7} & \textbf{1.463} \\
0.50 & 99.3 & 13.0 & 1.870 \\
1.00 & 97.4 & 2.6 & 1.974 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

At $T = 0.10$, the validation set achieved 92.0\% coverage with 53.7\% singletons. The held-out calibration set produced a \textbf{69.1\% singleton rate} with 86.4\% coverage; the test set confirmed at \textbf{67.7\% singletons and 84.3\% coverage}. The coverage gap (84.3\% vs.\ the 90\% target) indicates that $T = 0.10$ is slightly aggressive; a more conservative criterion (e.g., requiring 93\% validation coverage) would address this at the cost of fewer singletons. The guarantee holds provided the temperature is optimized on a validation split separate from the calibration set and the calibration set remains exchangeable with the test data~\cite{kofman2025}. Our 60/15/15/10 split satisfies both conditions.

\subsection{From Offline Calibration to Live Deployment}

Three adjustments were needed when deploying the conformal predictor in the live cascade. Table~\ref{tab:conformal-deployment} contrasts the configurations.

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\small
\caption{Conformal prediction: offline evaluation vs.\ live deployment.}
\label{tab:conformal-deployment}
\begin{tabular}{@{}lrr@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Parameter} & \textbf{Offline (V5)} & \textbf{Deployed} \\
\midrule
ConfTS temperature & 0.10 & 0.95 \\
APS threshold & 1.00 & 0.95 \\
Input graph & Full function graph & Full CPG (max 300 nodes) \\
Cal singleton rate & 69.1\% & n/a \\
Live GNN resolution & n/a & 2\% \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\textbf{Threshold 1.0 $\rightarrow$ 0.95.} For 2-class softmax with finite logits, $P(\text{top class}) < 1.0$ strictly; setting the threshold to 0.95 permits singleton predictions when the model assigns $\geq$95\% probability to one class.

\textbf{Temperature 0.10 $\rightarrow$ 0.95.} At $T = 0.10$, aggressive sharpening produced near-binary outputs on \emph{all} inputs, making every prediction a singleton regardless of actual uncertainty. At $T = 0.95$ (mild sharpening), the model preserves its natural confidence distribution, routing genuinely uncertain predictions to the LLM.

\textbf{Full CPG input.} The live pipeline's backward slicer reduced CPGs to 1--6 nodes (83--95\% node reduction), creating a distribution shift. Passing the full CPG with \texttt{max\_nodes=300} truncation aligned inference-time inputs with training conditions.

These adjustments illustrate a general challenge in deploying conformal prediction: offline calibration parameters do not transfer directly when the input distribution shifts. The cascade absorbs this gracefully. Even when the GNN resolves only 2\% of findings in live operation (compared to 69\% offline), the SAST pre-screener has already resolved 85\%, and the LLM handles the remaining 13\%. The conformal layer functions correctly: it routes uncertain predictions to the stage best equipped to handle them.

\section{\uppercase{End-to-End Cascade and Comparative Context}}
\label{sec:cascade-eval}

We evaluated the full cascade on 15 open-source repositories spanning five languages (Python, JavaScript, Java, C/C++, Go), producing 184 findings. This evaluation measures triage efficiency (what fraction resolves at each stage) rather than detection precision, since ground-truth labeling of 184 findings across diverse codebases was infeasible within the project timeline. Table~\ref{tab:cascade-results} reports per-stage resolution.

\textbf{SAST dominance (85\%).} The uncertainty scorer assigned $U < 0.5$ to 157 of 184 findings, resolving well-characterized patterns (SQL injection via string concatenation, hardcoded credentials, known-weak cryptographic algorithms) with static analysis alone.

\textbf{GNN contribution (2\%).} Only 4 findings produced conformal singleton sets, reflecting the conservative deployment temperature ($T = 0.95$) that deliberately preserved uncertainty.

\textbf{LLM handles the hard cases (13\%).} The 23 escalated findings required semantic reasoning: \texttt{eval()} calls guarded by regex allowlists, deserialization of hardcoded local files, and command injection via UUID-generated paths. The dual-agent consensus protocol produced verdicts for all 23 with zero timeouts or parse failures.

\textbf{Cost analysis.} The cascade required 23 LLM API calls instead of 184, an \textbf{87.5\% reduction}. At production scales (1,000+ findings per scan), this translates to \$0.50--2.00 versus \$10--40 per scan for a full-LLM approach. The cascade also naturally respects Gemini free-tier rate limits (15 RPM), since only 13\% of findings reach Stage~3. Total wall-clock time across all 15 repositories was approximately 1,178 seconds (19.6 minutes), with variance dominated by Stage~3 latency (5--10 seconds per LLM call).

\textbf{CWE-adaptive fusion.} All 184 findings received fused confidence scores using CWE-adaptive weights. Injection CWEs used LLM-heavy weights ($\alpha = 0.25, \beta = 0.25, \gamma = 0.50$), memory CWEs used graph-heavy weights ($\alpha = 0.20, \beta = 0.50, \gamma = 0.30$), and crypto CWEs used SAST-heavy weights ($\alpha = 0.50, \beta = 0.20, \gamma = 0.30$). Classification thresholds ($\geq 0.85$ CONFIRMED, $\geq 0.50$ LIKELY, $< 0.50$ POTENTIAL) produced a distribution of verdicts allowing each vulnerability class to leverage the stage best equipped to analyze it.

\textbf{Comparative context.} The proposed framework is a cascade, not a SAST tool, so comparing its F1 to any single tool's F1 is a category error; the numbers below are drawn from respective publications under different conditions and are not directly comparable. A controlled head-to-head evaluation on OWASP Benchmark v1.2 is planned as future work. SAST tools (Semgrep, CodeQL, SonarQube) suffer false positive rates of 68--95\% on realistic codebases~\cite{ghostsecurity2025}; published FP rates are Semgrep 74.8\%, CodeQL 68.2\%, SonarQube 94.6\%, and developers abandon tools above 30--50\%~\cite{johnson2013}. Our cascade consumes these tools as Stage~1 input and applies two filtering stages. GNN-based detectors (Devign, ReGVD, LineVul) achieve F1 of 0.64--0.72 on standard benchmarks, but these metrics collapse on realistic data~\cite{primevul2024}; our MiniGINv3 wraps the GNN in a conformal layer that quantifies when predictions should be trusted, a capability absent from published GNN detectors. LLM-augmented systems (IRIS~\cite{iris2025}, ZeroFalse) process every finding through an LLM at prohibitive cost; our cascade reduces LLM invocations by 87.5\%, resolving 85\% of findings at the cheapest stage while directing semantic analysis only where needed.

\section{\uppercase{Discussion}}
\label{sec:discussion}

We identify four threats to validity. Our metrics capture classification accuracy and cost efficiency but not deployment utility such as triage time or cognitive burden (\emph{construct validity})~\cite{johnson2013}. Data leakage may inflate GNN results, since BigVul, DiverseVul, and Devign draw from overlapping C/C++ projects, and MD5 deduplication eliminates exact but not semantic near-duplicates; PrimeVul~\cite{primevul2024} showed rigorous deduplication can collapse F1 by 65+ points, and the conformal coverage guarantee assumes exchangeability that does not formally hold in live deployment (\emph{internal validity}). The training corpus is 94.6\% C/C++, Python F1 of 0.836 rests on only $\sim$60 test samples, JavaScript/Java/Go results are statistically unreliable at $<$30 samples each, the 15 live repositories were not formally sampled, and the LLM component depends on Gemini 2.5 Flash whose behavior may change with model updates (\emph{external validity}). LLM validation is non-deterministic with 47--76\% inconsistency in repeated tasks~\cite{steenhoek2024}; we report single-run results, the dual-agent protocol partially mitigates this by requiring attacker-defender agreement, and the 184-finding evaluation lacks ground truth, so we measure triage efficiency but not detection accuracy (\emph{reliability}).

\textbf{Research questions.} \textbf{RQ1}: the SAST pre-screener resolved 85\% of findings using uncertainty scoring ($U < 0.5$), confirming that four-factor uncertainty enables cost-effective triage. \textbf{RQ2}: the APS layer produces 69.1\% singletons offline and functions as a conservative routing gate in deployment. \textbf{RQ3/RQ4}: dual-agent consensus and CWE-adaptive fusion are architectural contributions validated through successful integration in the live cascade (23 findings processed with zero failures); controlled ablation comparing single-agent vs.\ dual-agent and fixed-weight vs.\ adaptive-weight configurations represents the natural next evaluation step. \textbf{RQ5}: the 87.5\% LLM call reduction demonstrates a measurable cost advantage over non-cascaded alternatives.

Three cross-cutting insights emerged. First, classification and calibration have conflicting requirements: label smoothing improves F1 but destroys the logit separation that conformal prediction needs. Second, offline conformal calibration does not transfer to live deployment; the 69.1\% singleton rate dropped to 2\% due to threshold boundaries, temperature sharpening, and input distribution shift, suggesting that conformal systems require deployment-specific recalibration. Third, cascade economics dominate: even if the GNN contributed zero resolution, the SAST pre-screener alone would reduce LLM calls by 85\%, making the cost advantage robust to individual component performance. These results inform the conclusions and future directions in Chapter~5.
